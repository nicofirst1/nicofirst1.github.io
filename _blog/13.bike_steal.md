> **TL;DR;** I spent seven months recording the visible state of my building's shared combination lock. Using nothing but frequency analysis and bootstrapping, I recovered 3 out of 4 digits of the true code. The fourth digit is protected by an accident of thumb physics. Your 10,000-combination lock? It's really a 100-combination lock.


## The Scene of the Crime

I live in an apartment building in Bonn. In the basement there's a shared bike storage room, locked with a 4-digit rotary combination lock. Four wheels, each showing a digit from 0 to 9, arranged horizontally. About 20 households share this lock. You spin the wheels, open, park your bike, scramble the digits, leave.

<img src="/assets/images/blog/bike_steal/bike_room.jpg" alt="The bike room" width="500"/>

10,000 possible combinations. Should be safe, right? 
![surprise](/assets/images/blog/bike_steal/padme_anakin.jpg)


Last year my building had a series of break-ins[^breakin]. Someone got into the common areas, the private garages, even stole a couple of electric bikes from locked parking spots. But the bike room? Untouched. At first I was relieved. Then I started wondering: what if the thieves came back? What if they were patient enough to check the lock every now and then, write down the digits, and eventually figure out the code?

[^breakin]: We also had a break-in in our apartment that year. It was rough. On the bright side, it eventually led to us getting our two cats, Alba and Nera. <img src="/assets/images/blog/bike_steal/cats.jpg" alt="Alba and Nera" width="200" style="display:block;margin-top:0.5rem;border-radius:4px;"/> 

Because every time someone opens that lock and scrambles it, the digits they leave behind aren't random. They're the product of a quick thumb swipe across a physical mechanism. And humans are terrible at being random[^random].

[^random]: Figurska, Stańczyk & Kulesza (2008), "Humans cannot consciously generate random numbers sequences." Published in *Medical Hypotheses*. Best paper title in the history of science.

So I did what any reasonable person would do. I started writing down the numbers. 🕵️

Every time I went to the bike room, morning or evening, I recorded the four digits visible on the lock before entering the code. I kept this up from August 2025 through March 2026. Seven months. ~100 observations[^gym].

[^gym]: I use the bike mostly to go to the gym, which means the data collection was... let's say, seasonal. There are some heroic stretches of daily observations and some long, suspiciously lazy gaps. The data reflects my fitness motivation more than my scientific discipline.

## The Naive Attack

A thief walks up to your lock. They've been watching. What do they see?

### The Wheels
Let's start simple. Each wheel can show any digit from 0 to 9, and across ~100 observations, some digits show up a lot more than others. The question is: *how much more?*

For each of the four positions, I counted how often each digit appeared. The result is below, shown as four polar bar charts, one per wheel. Think of each chart as a top-down view of the physical wheel: digits 0 through 9 arranged clockwise around the circle, with bar length showing how often that digit was observed. The faint inner ring marks 10%, which is what you'd expect if scrambling were truly random[^uniform].

[^uniform]: If every digit were equally likely after scrambling, each would appear 10% of the time. That ring is your null hypothesis, staring back at you.

<!-- VIZ: "The Wheels" — Four polar bar charts, horizontal row, one per position -->
<div data-viz="wheels" style="margin: 1.5rem 0; min-height: 180px;"></div>

Take a moment to look at them.

Position 1 has a clear winner. One digit towers above the rest. Position 4 is similar, with a strong favourite. Position 3 is more contested, two digits fighting for the top spot. And position 2? Position 2 is a mess. There's a vague winner, but nothing that screams confidence.

If you were a thief and could only look at these charts, you'd pick the tallest bar at each position and call it your guess. That's the **naive frequency attack**: just go with the mode (the most frequent digit).


### Now Guess
So let's do exactly that. Pick the mode at each position. You get: **4-4-4-7**.

<!-- VIZ: "The Guess" — Four wheel icons showing guessed digit -->
<div data-viz="guess" style="margin: 1.5rem 0; min-height: 120px;"></div>

Three out of four. Not bad for staring at histograms.

Positions 1, 3, and 4 are correct. Position 2 is wrong: the mode says 4, but the truth is 7. In practice, this means a thief using this strategy has one digit to figure out. Lock in the three confident positions, try all 10 options for the remaining one, and you're done in under a minute.

But hold on. Are those three "correct" guesses equally trustworthy? Position 1's mode dominated its chart. Position 3 barely won a two-horse race. Should we really trust them the same way?

Spoiler: no. And that's where things get interesting.


## How Confident Should You Be?

Not all three correct guesses are equally reliable. Position 1's mode appeared 27 times out of ~100, with the runner-up at just 14. Position 3's mode got 20, the runner-up 17. That's a gap of 3 observations. A couple of different neighbours on a couple of different days, and the ranking could flip.

So how do we figure out which guesses to trust?

### Bootstraping!
Here's a thought experiment. Imagine you have a bag with 100 marbles, and each marble has a digit written on it, matching one of your observations for a given position. You shake the bag, pull out a marble, write down the digit, and put it back[^replacement]. Then you do it again. 100 times total.

[^replacement]: This "put it back" part is important. It means the same observation can be picked more than once in a single draw, and some observations won't be picked at all. This is what statisticians call sampling *with replacement*. It's the key ingredient that lets you simulate variability from a single dataset.

Now you look at your 100 drawn digits and ask: what's the mode? Is it the same digit that won in the original data?

Maybe. Maybe not. Some marbles got picked twice, others got skipped. The histogram looks slightly different every time. If the original winner had a big lead (like position 1, where 4 leads), it will almost certainly still win. If it was close (like position 3), a few unlucky draws and the runner-up takes over.

That's [bootstrapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bootstrapping_(statistics)). You repeat this process thousands of times (I did it 100,000 times) and count: in how many of those simulated experiments does the original mode still come out on top? That percentage is your **bootstrap stability**, a measure of how much you should trust the guess.


### Try It Yourself

Don't take my word for it.

<!-- VIZ: "The Resampler" — Interactive bootstrap visualization -->
<div data-viz="resampler" style="margin: 1.5rem 0; min-height: 160px;"></div>

Click "Resample" three times. Position 1 is absurd: digit 4 wins 96.4% of resamples, with a **gap ratio**[^gapratio] of 54x over the runner-up. It doesn't even flinch. Position 4 is solid too: 7 takes it 81% of the time, *gap ratio* 5.1x.

[^gapratio]: The gap ratio is how many times more often the winner beats the runner-up across resamples. A gap of 54x means digit 4 won 54 times more often than the second-place digit. In the resampler, it's shown as a bracket between the top two bars in each panel.

Now look at position 3. Digit 4 wins 62% of resamples, but digit 1 is right there at 27%, breathing down its neck. The *gap ratio* is just 2.3x. This guess is *probably* right, but it would take a few hundred more observations to feel truly confident.

And position 2? It looks stable on the surface: digit 4 wins 67.5% of resamples with a *gap ratio* of 4.9x. Seems fine, right? But the true digit is 7. Poor 7. It wins just **0.7%** of resamples. The bootstrap is very confident here. **Confidently wrong.**

We'll come back to that.

For now, the takeaway: positions 1 and 4 are cracked. Position 3 is likely cracked but shaky. And position 2 is telling us a convincing lie.

So where does this leave our thief?


## The Attacker's Playbook

Let's think like a thief for a moment.

You've been watching the lock. You've done the frequency analysis. You know positions 1 and 4 are almost certainly 4 and 7. Position 2 looks solid: digit 4 wins 67.5% of resamples with a *gap ratio* of 4.9x. Position 3 is your weakest link: digit 4 wins 62% with a gap of just 2.3x.

A naive thief tries 4-4-4-7 and either gets lucky or doesn't. A *smart* thief reasons differently: lock in the two high-confidence digits (positions 1 and 4), and brute-force the rest. Positions 2 and 3 each have 10 possible digits. That's 10 × 10 = 100 combinations to try.

Your 10,000-combination lock just became a 100-combination lock.

But an even smarter thief doesn't try them randomly. They **rank** the 100 codes by likelihood and work down the list. Attempt #1: 4-4-4-7 (the all-modes guess). Attempt #2: swap in position 3's runner-up: 4-4-1-7. And so on. Each attempt takes maybe 3 to 5 seconds on a rotary lock. If the true code is near the top of the ranked list, you're done in under a minute. Even in the worst case, 100 attempts is 5 to 8 minutes.

### The Search Space

Here's what the thief's ranked list looks like.

To see why this works, let's look at the full space of 10,000 possible codes, ranked by how likely they are under our frequency model[^frequency]. The green bars are codes inside the attack zone (everything matching 4-?-?-7). The gray bars are everything else. You'll notice some gray codes rank higher than many green ones. That's because a code like 4-4-4-0 can still score well if its individual digits are frequent, even though it falls outside the attack zone.

[^frequency]: The likelihood of a code is the product of per-position observed frequencies. For code 4-4-4-7, you multiply the observed frequency of digit 4 at position 1 × digit 4 at position 2 × digit 4 at position 3 × digit 7 at position 4.

<div data-viz="searchspace" style="margin: 1.5rem 0; min-height: 460px;"></div>

The true code, 4-7-4-7, ranks **#23** out of 10,000 by likelihood. But that rank isn't stable: across bootstrap resamples, it bounces around quite a bit[^median_rank]. What matters is that about **73%** of the time, the true code falls within the top 100. The attack zone works.

[^median_rank]: Median rank: 44. Mean: 105. 95% CI: [3, 574]. Sometimes the true code is in the top 10, sometimes it's in the hundreds. About 54% of the time it's in the top 50.

So here's the recipe for our thief. Observe the lock about 100 times. Pick the mode at positions 1 and 4. Rank the remaining 100 combinations by frequency. Start at the top. On a good day, you're in within a couple of minutes.


## The Stubborn Digit

This is the part that kept me up at night.

Position 2's true digit is 7. In our data, it appears 9.3% of the time. That's *below* the 10% you'd expect from perfectly random scrambling. The mode, digit 4, appears at roughly 20%. Not only does the true digit lose, it loses to random chance.

And here's the kicker: **more data makes this worse, not better.**

To understand why, I ran a simulation. Take the observed frequency distribution at position 2 and use it as ground truth. Draw N synthetic observations from it, and look at the frequencies of digit 4 and digit 7. Repeat 20,000 times. Do this for N = 10, 25, 50, 100, all the way up to 5,000.

This is the same bootstrapping idea from before, just with a dial for sample size.

### The Divergence

The chart below shows two lines: the observed frequency of digit 4 (the wrong mode, in red) and digit 7 (the true code, in green). The shaded bands are the 95% confidence intervals at each sample size. Drag the slider to change N.

<div data-viz="divergence" style="margin: 1.5rem 0; min-height: 400px;"></div>

Here's what you'll notice. The gap between the two lines never changes. It's always about 10.3 percentage points, at N=10 and at N=5,000. What changes is the uncertainty around each line. At small N, the bands are wide and overlapping. You can't tell the two digits apart. Maybe digit 7 really is more common and you just got unlucky. There's hope.

Drag the slider right. The bands shrink. By N=100 (roughly what I collected), they barely touch. By N=500, they're fully separated. There's no overlap. No ambiguity. Digit 4 is observed more often than digit 7, and it's not even close.

The gap was always there. More data doesn't close it. It just proves it's real.

A thief looking at this data would have no reason to doubt the conclusion. Every statistical test would confirm it. Digit 4 appears twice as often as digit 7 at position 2. Pick 4. Move on. **And they'd be wrong.**

This is not a sample-size problem. The scrambling process at position 2 actively buries the true digit under a false favourite. Three out of four positions are recoverable with enough patience. But the fourth is protected.

Which brings us to the obvious question: why? What is it about position 2 that makes the data lie?


## The Physics of a Thumb

I'm fairly sure *Thumb Physics* is not an established branch of science. If it is, I'd like credit for arriving at it independently. If it isn't, I'm claiming it. Put it on my tombstone.

Anyway. Let's step back from the statistics for a moment.

The lock is horizontal. Four wheels in a row. When someone scrambles it, they don't carefully rotate each wheel independently. They swipe a thumb across all four wheels in one motion, usually left to right.

This simple physical fact explains a lot. But not everything.

### Inner vs Outer

The outer wheels (positions 1 and 4) sit at the edges of the thumb's arc. They get clipped, nudged, sometimes skipped entirely. The inner wheels (positions 2 and 3) are right in the middle of the swipe. They get the full force of the rotation.

The data backs this up. Outer positions stay unchanged (showing the true digit) roughly 30% of the time. Inner positions only about 10 to 15%[^fisher]. The difference is statistically significant. Your thumb simply doesn't reach the edges as well as the middle.

[^fisher]: Fisher's exact test gives p=0.011 for the inner/outer asymmetry. Significant at the 5% level.

This explains why positions 1 and 4 are easy to crack: they don't get scrambled enough, so the true digit still shows through in the frequency data. But it doesn't explain position 2's specific problem. Why does digit 4 dominate there when the true digit is 7?

### The Mystery of Position 2

I had a beautiful theory for this: the "neighbour effect." Position 2 sits between two 4s, so maybe the thumb leaks the neighbours' digit into it. Elegant! Then I checked position 3, which sits between two 7s. Its mode is 4, not 7. Theory dead on arrival.

Here's what we actually know. Digit 4 is the most common digit at three out of four positions. Across the entire lock, it appears at 19.1%. It's not just popular at position 2. It's popular *everywhere*. It seems to be generically "sticky" on this lock.

Why? I don't have a satisfying answer. It could be something about the physics of this particular lock, or of rotary locks in general, that we can't disentangle with one lock and 100 observations.

The data shows the pattern clearly. The mechanism remains a mystery.

## So, Can You Steal My Bike?

Probably. Yes.

Positions 1 and 4 are trivially recoverable. The true digits dominate the frequency data from as few as 25 observations. Position 3 is recoverable but shaky, it would benefit from a few hundred more observations to feel truly confident. Position 2 is protected by a statistical accident: the true digit appears below random chance, and more data only makes the wrong answer louder.

Your 10,000-combination lock provides the security of about 100 combinations. A patient observer with ~100 data points (seven months of gym trips, in my case) can rank those 100 by likelihood and brute-force through them in a few minutes. About 73% of the time, the true code is in there.

Or you could just cut the lock. Probably faster.

The dataset is available at the end of this post if you want to replicate the analysis or try to do better than naive frequency. I'm still collecting data[^stillcollecting]. And no, I haven't changed the code. For science.
